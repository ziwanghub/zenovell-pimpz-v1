# PHASE 6D — SECTION 4 MOBILE VISUAL PRIORITY AUTHORITY V2

## 1. Executive Summary

This document defines the visual attention authority for Section 4 Mobile.

Its purpose is to remove the final ambiguity before Blueprint V2 by establishing:

- what users should notice first
- what users should notice second
- what should remain supportive
- what must stay quiet
- what may never compete for dominance

This is not a styling document.

This is not a measurement document.

This is not an implementation document.

This document exists so Blueprint V2 can translate the approved business, UX, decision, layout, and wireframe authority into a visual hierarchy system without reinterpretation.

---

## 2. Visual Attention Model

### Primary Attention

Primary attention is reserved for the content that drives product choice immediately.

Primary attention belongs to:

- product fit
- product title
- strongest product difference

Primary attention answers:

- what is this
- is it relevant to me
- why is it different

### Secondary Attention

Secondary attention supports commitment once relevance is established.

Secondary attention belongs to:

- price
- card CTA
- product image

Secondary attention answers:

- is this worth considering
- what does it cost
- can I act now

### Supporting Attention

Supporting attention helps reduce hesitation but must not interrupt decision-making.

Supporting attention belongs to:

- benefit row
- trust layer
- bottom CTA

Supporting attention answers:

- what confirms this choice
- is this safe
- what if I still need help

### Background Attention

Background attention is reserved for low-priority closure or ornamental support.

Background attention belongs to:

- footer note
- decorative emphasis that does not improve choice quality

Background attention should never redirect users away from the decision task.

---

## 3. Attention Journey

### First 500ms

Users should notice:

- that this is a product-choice section
- that multiple products are available
- that the section is structured, not chaotic

### First 1 Second

Users should notice:

- the top row of products
- that each card is a distinct option
- the strongest relevance signals

### First 2 Seconds

Users should notice:

- which products look most relevant
- the names of likely-fit candidates
- that price and action exist but are not the first thing to solve

### First 3 Seconds

Users should notice:

- which one or two products deserve closer attention
- what the most obvious differences are
- that the section supports confident choice

### Final Decision Moment

Before action, users should feel visual certainty that:

- they understand the product well enough
- the product feels relevant
- the price is clear
- CTA is the next logical step

---

## 4. Attention Competition Rules

### What May Compete

The following may share controlled competition within a card:

- fit and title
- title and difference
- price and CTA

This competition is acceptable because these elements belong to the same decision path.

### What May Never Compete

The following must never compete equally:

- bottom CTA versus card CTA
- trust layer versus product grid
- footer note versus decision content
- decorative energy versus fit clarity
- benefit detail versus core difference
- image drama versus product meaning

### Competition Rule

Only adjacent decision layers may share attention.

Support and background layers must never compete with the main choice task.

## 5. Card Attention Model

### Image

- Attention priority: secondary
- Role: opens recognition and emotional interest
- Rule: should attract, but not dominate interpretation

### Fit

- Attention priority: primary
- Role: earliest relevance signal
- Rule: must receive stronger attention than benefits

### Title

- Attention priority: primary
- Role: identity anchor
- Rule: must feel immediately connected to fit

### Difference

- Attention priority: primary
- Role: comparison accelerator
- Rule: should reduce ambiguity before deep reading

### Benefits

- Attention priority: supporting
- Role: confirmation
- Rule: must not become the card’s main explanation layer

### Price

- Attention priority: secondary
- Role: commitment trigger
- Rule: must become visible after relevance, not before it

### CTA

- Attention priority: secondary-high
- Role: action completion
- Rule: should feel available without overpowering unclear cards

### Card Attention Rule

The card must visually move from:

recognition

to

relevance

to

difference

to

commitment

to

action

---

## 6. Section Attention Model

### Intro

- Attention level: primary at entry
- Role: orient the user toward product selection
- Rule: should open the section, then release attention to the grid

### Grid

- Attention level: dominant
- Role: main decision workspace
- Rule: must absorb most attention in the section

### Trust

- Attention level: supporting
- Role: reassurance after comparison
- Rule: should calm risk, not redirect attention

### Bottom CTA

- Attention level: supporting
- Role: fallback action for unresolved users
- Rule: must not compete with already-made product decisions

### Footer

- Attention level: background
- Role: quiet brand closure
- Rule: should finish the section without calling users back into evaluation

- guide choice
- reduce hesitation
- support fast recognition

### Medium Emphasis

Used for:

- price
- CTA

Purpose:

- support commitment
- support action readiness
- sustain product desirability

### Low Emphasis

- benefits
- trust

- footer

Purpose:

- maintain finish
- avoid visual shouting

---

## 9. Premium Attention Philosophy

Premium interfaces avoid visual shouting because confidence depends on controlled emphasis.

When every element asks for attention:

- choice becomes harder
- trust becomes weaker
- premium perception drops

When attention is controlled:

- users feel guided
- meaning arrives faster
- action feels more intentional
- the interface feels more expensive because it feels more certain

### Premium Attention Rules

- the most important thing should be clearly the most important thing
- supporting information should know its place
- decorative energy should never outrank decision clarity
- reassurance should feel calm, not insistent
- action should feel earned, not pushed
